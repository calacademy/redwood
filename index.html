Biography of a Redwood

Every tree, like every human, is totally unique. A tree’s growth rings can record many events in its life, and for coast redwoods, that life can last 2,000 years or more. Their rings give us a window into California’s past that may help us understand our future.

A Redwood Grows Up

Around the year 1200, this tree took root in the temperate rainforest we now call Prairie Creek Redwoods State Park. Every year, it added a new layer of wood, growing outward and upward over time.

Seasons of Growth

From early spring through summer, redwoods grow as vigorously as they can, creating the larger, lighter portion of each annual ring. The darker, thinner section of each ring forms from late summer to fall, as growth slows for winter.

False Ring

Redwoods occasionally produce a “false ring” within a true ring boundary. For example, early arrival of unseasonal, winter-like conditions could leave an inconsistent dark line where a tree’s growth slowed, but then briefly resumed high wood production before the true winter began.

Reading into Ring Widths

Our tree grew normally in 1726, but a few years later in 1729, its growth ring is thinner. Lack of water likely caused poor growth that year during a drought that stretched across the western United States.

Years of Plenty

Growing seasons with more water, nutrients, and light generally produce thicker growth rings. But a tree’s center rings are thicker compared to its outer rings, regardless of growing conditions. Because of this size-related geometry, the significance of ring width is best judged by comparing other rings nearby.

Water Over Time

This tree sprouted during a 175-year megadrought in the western United States—a climate history discovered in part by studying tree rings. This tree’s location in Northern California’s temperate rainforest likely helped sustain it with winter rainfall supplemented by summer fog.

A Puzzle in Rings

Scientist Allyson Carroll has measured more than a million tree rings. Known as dendrochronology, the study of tree rings rewards patience – particularly in coast redwoods.

Carroll scanned and measured all of the rings on this round, working around inconsistent wood growth where rings wedge together or are missing entirely. By matching ring-width patterns to those of other redwood trees in the Prairie Creek forest, she pieced together accurate dates for individual growth rings on this redwood round.

Internal Struggle

This redwood tree survived for more than 800 years, but along the way, a parasitic fungus managed to gain a foothold. It created this dark pocket of rot within the tree’s strong heartwood.

Fighting Fungus

Bright-red compounds called tannins—toxic to many insects and microbes—give redwoods their famous color and resistance to most pests. Only a few species, like the Poria sequoiae that caused this decay, can get past the redwood’s chemical defenses.

Strength Under Fire

In 1714, a large fire burned through the Prairie Creek forest, leaving scars on many trees—including this dark crevice. Its cause is unknown. The redwood’s thick, fibrous bark likely protected its wood from smaller fires, but this large fire left a mark.

Natural Causes

The Yurok Tribe has used low-intensity fires to maintain healthy redwood forests for centuries, but their controlled burns likely did not cause this particular fire. It burned hot and high enough to cause damage 154 feet above the ground, suggesting it was an uncontrolled natural wildfire.

Down, But Not Out

A severe storm barreling through the forest can break off limbs or sections of trunk. Protruding areas of wood like this, called burl, allow redwoods to weather such storms. Bud tissue stored within the burl can sprout new, genetically identical tree trunks—even if the parent tree has fallen over.

Benefits of Burl

This ability to regenerate helps redwoods survive for thousands of years. Over time, the process of damage and regrowth creates a highly complex and totally unique crown in each mature redwood tree.

Tall Drink of Fog

In dry years, redwoods may rely more on Northern California’s summer fog. Fog water collects on the leaves and drips to the ground, supplying moisture to the tree and nearby understory plants. Most plants take in water through their roots, but redwood leaves also absorb fog water directly.

Invisible Insights

You can’t see the relative amounts of rainwater and fog water in a tree ring just by looking at it. However, fog water has a different chemical signature than rainwater. Measuring the chemical composition of the rings can provide insight into how redwoods use these two water sources under different climate conditions.

Heavyweights of Carbon Capture

A powerful storm toppled this giant in February 2017. The whole tree weighed close to half a million pounds, and this piece came close to the 9,700-pound weight limit of the helicopter that lifted it out of the forest.

To make all that wood, the tree pulled many tons of carbon out of the atmosphere. In fact, redwood forests store more carbon than any other forest on Earth. Because redwoods resist decay, the carbon they capture during their lives stays locked in their wood for centuries after their death, helping keep our climate in balance.

Clues to Climate

Earth’s natural climate variation often unfolds over centuries and millennia—a timescale far beyond a human lifespan, but well within a redwood lifespan. This tree lived through several natural climate fluctuations and the start of human-caused climate change.

Local growth

Climate affects redwood growth, but local factors may play a larger role in an individual tree’s growth. A fallen neighbor tree may suddenly have provided this tree with more light, for example. We can see larger patterns of climatic impact on coast redwood growth when we compare the rings of many trees.
